Towards Empowerment Gain through Causal Structure Learning in Model-Based RL

Anonymous submission
ICLR 2025

Abstract

Figure 1: (a). Example of robot manipulation task with three trajectories and three nodes: one target node (movable) and two noisy nodes (one movable, one unmovable). (b). Underlying causal structures of the example.


Empowerment and causal reasoning are crucial abilities for intelligence. Empowerment enhances the ability of agents to actively control their environments by maximizing the mutual information between future states and actions. In Model-Based Reinforcement Learning (MBRL), incorporating causal structures into dynamics models provides agents with a structured understanding of the environments, enabling better outcomes. We posit that empowerment coupled with causal understanding can improve controllability, while enhanced empowerment gain can further facilitate causal reasoning in MBRL. To improve learning efficiency and controllability, we propose a novel framework, Empowerment through Causal Learning (ECL), where an agent with the awareness of causal dynamics models achieves empowerment-driven exploration and utilizes its structured causal perception and control for task learning. Specifically, ECL operates by first training a causal dynamics model of the environment based on collected data. We then maximize empowerment under the causal structure for policy learning, simultaneously updating the causal dynamics model to be more controllable than dense dynamics model without causal structure. Moreover, an intrinsic curiosity reward is also included to balance the causality, mitigating overfitting during policy learning. Importantly, our framework is method-agnostic and is capable of integrating various causal discovery and RL algorithms. We evaluate ECL combined with different causal discovery methods across $6$ environments including pixel-based tasks, demonstrating its superior performance compared to other causal MBRL methods, in terms of causal discovery, sample efficiency, and asymptotic performance.
